The Three Pillars · तीन स्तंभ

Foundations of Indian Law

India's legal system blends constitutional supremacy, statutory law, and judicial precedent — rooted in the principle of Dharma as the highest law.

📜
The Constitution
संविधान · 1950

The supreme law of Bharat, adopted on 26 January 1950. It guarantees Fundamental Rights (Articles 12–35), Directive Principles (Articles 36–51), and Fundamental Duties (Article 51A). Every other law must conform to it — it is the Dharmashastra of modern India.

⚖️
Statutory Law
संसदीय कानून

Acts passed by Parliament and State Legislatures. Key codes include the Indian Penal Code (IPC/BNS 2023), Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC/BNSS 2023), Civil Procedure Code (CPC), Indian Evidence Act, and hundreds of special acts covering family, property, labour, and commerce.

🏛️
Judiciary
न्यायपालिका

An independent three-tier structure: the Supreme Court at the apex (Article 124), 25 High Courts at state level (Article 214), and subordinate District & Sessions Courts below. The Supreme Court's decisions bind all lower courts under Article 141.

Essential Acts · प्रमुख कानून

Key Laws Every Citizen Should Know

India has modernised its core criminal codes in 2023. Here is an overview of the laws most relevant to everyday life and justice-seeking.

📖 Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023 — New IPC +

Replaces the Indian Penal Code (1860) from 1 July 2024. Defines offences and punishments covering crimes against the state, human body, property, public order, and more.

  • Offences against women and children given highest priority with enhanced penalties
  • New offences: terrorism, organised crime, hit-and-run, and snatching defined explicitly
  • Community service introduced as a punishment for minor first-time offences
  • Sedition replaced by a broader "acts endangering sovereignty" provision
  • Contains 358 sections (vs 511 in old IPC)
🔍 Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) 2023 — New CrPC +

Replaces the Code of Criminal Procedure (1973). Governs the procedural law for investigation, trial, bail, and sentencing.

  • Mandatory videography of crime scenes and search-and-seizure operations
  • Zero FIR: You can file an FIR at any police station regardless of jurisdiction
  • Timeline mandates for trials: sessions court must complete within 3 years
  • Electronic summons and notices are now legally valid
  • Victim's right to be heard during plea bargaining and before closure of cases
📋 Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA) 2023 — New Evidence Act +

Replaces the Indian Evidence Act (1872). Governs what can be admitted as evidence in courts.

  • Electronic records including emails, WhatsApp messages, digital documents now explicitly admissible
  • Electronic and digital records treated on par with paper documents
  • Streamlined provisions for secondary evidence and certified copies
  • Provisions for joint trials and confessions updated
👨‍👩‍👧 Family Law — Personal Laws & Hindu Code +

India has religion-specific personal laws for marriage, divorce, succession, and adoption.

  • Hindu Marriage Act 1955 — Marriage, divorce, maintenance, restitution of conjugal rights
  • Hindu Succession Act 1956 (amended 2005) — Daughters have equal coparcenary rights
  • Hindu Adoption and Maintenance Act 1956
  • Special Marriage Act 1954 — Civil marriage for all religions
  • Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act 2005 — Civil remedies against abuse
  • Dowry Prohibition Act 1961 — Giving or taking dowry is criminal
🏠 Property & Land Law +

Property rights in India are governed by a combination of central and state laws.

  • Transfer of Property Act 1882 — Rules for sale, mortgage, lease, gift, and exchange
  • Registration Act 1908 — Mandatory registration of sale deeds and certain other documents
  • RERA 2016 — Real Estate (Regulation & Development) Act protects home buyers from developers
  • Land Acquisition Act 2013 — Fair compensation and rehabilitation when land is acquired by the state
  • Benami Transactions (Prohibition) Act — Prohibits holding property in another's name
🛡️ Consumer & Citizen Rights +

Laws that protect citizens from exploitation by businesses and the state.

  • Consumer Protection Act 2019 — File complaints against defective goods, deficient services, unfair trade; e-commerce platforms now included
  • Right to Information Act 2005 — Any citizen can seek information from government offices within 30 days
  • POSH Act 2013 — Protection of Women from Sexual Harassment at the workplace
  • SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act 1989 — Special protections for scheduled castes and tribes
  • Persons with Disabilities Act 2016 — Rights and protections for differently-abled persons

Your Legal Journey · न्याय की राह

How to Seek Justice — Step by Step

Whether you face a criminal matter or a civil dispute, the Indian legal system provides a clear path. Know your steps.

Know Your Rights

Understand which law applies to your situation. Every citizen has the right to free legal aid (Article 39A) if they cannot afford a lawyer. Legal Services Authorities at district level provide free counsel.

File a Complaint

For criminal matters, file an FIR at any police station (Zero FIR allowed under BNSS). For civil matters, file a plaint in the appropriate District Court. Consumer complaints go to District Consumer Commissions.

Court Process

Cases proceed through pleadings, evidence, and arguments. Lok Adalats offer fast out-of-court settlements with no court fees. Mediation centres and arbitration are available for civil and commercial disputes.

Appellate Remedy

Decisions of lower courts can be appealed upward — District Court → High Court → Supreme Court. Writ petitions under Articles 32 (SC) or 226 (HC) can directly challenge fundamental rights violations.

✦ Age One · प्रथम युग

Satya Yuga

सत्य युग · The Age of Truth & Pure Goodness

The first and greatest of the four ages. Dharma stands on all four legs — truth, compassion, austerity, and charity. Humans live for thousands of years, possess great spiritual power, and live in direct awareness of the Divine. There are no wars, no disease, no injustice. The very air is charged with truth.

🐟
Matsya AvatarVishnu descends as a great fish to save Manu (the first man) and the sacred Vedas from the cosmic deluge. The first Avatar — the preservation of knowledge against total dissolution.
🐢
Kurma AvatarVishnu as the cosmic tortoise supports Mount Mandara as the gods and asuras churn the cosmic ocean (Samudra Manthan) to obtain Amrita — the nectar of immortality. From this churning emerge all of creation's treasures.
🐗
Varaha AvatarThe demon Hiranyaksha submerges the Earth beneath the cosmic ocean. Vishnu descends as a colossal boar, slays Hiranyaksha in a cosmic battle, and lifts the Earth on his tusks — restoring dharmic order.
🦁
Narasimha & PrahladaThe tyrant Hiranyakashipu declares himself God and terrorises the three worlds. His own son Prahlada refuses to abandon his devotion to Vishnu. Vishnu descends as Narasimha (half-man, half-lion) and destroys the tyrant — vindicating Prahlada's courage and truth.
👣
Vamana & King BaliThe virtuous demon king Bali's power grows so vast it threatens cosmic balance. Vishnu descends as Vamana, a dwarf brahmin, and asks for three steps of land. In three strides he measures all the worlds — demonstrating that dharmic humility transcends even the greatest worldly power.
Moral Takeaway for Today

The Satya Yuga teaches that truth is not merely a virtue — it is the structural foundation of a just society. When institutions are built on truth (Satya), they endure; when they are built on convenience and power, they collapse. The courage of Prahlada — maintaining one's dharmic conviction even against the most powerful authority — mirrors the principle of judicial independence and the duty of a citizen to uphold the constitution against a tyrannical state.

🏹 Age Two · द्वितीय युग

Treta Yuga

त्रेता युग · The Age of Three-Fourths Dharma

The second age. Dharma stands on three legs; one quarter — dishonesty — has crept into the world. Good still dominates, but evil now exists and must be actively combated. Life spans shorten. The age produces India's first and most beloved grand epic: the Ramayana.

🏹
Birth of Lord RamaThe seventh Avatar of Vishnu, born as the prince of Ayodhya to King Dasharatha. The ideal human being — perfectly virtuous son, husband, king, and warrior. His life is the standard of dharmic conduct against which all rulers and persons are measured.
🌿
The 14-Year ExileBound by his father's oath and his own dharma as a son, Rama accepts exile without complaint. This episode encodes the principle that the ruler must be above personal interest and political self-preservation — a selfless servant of dharma even at personal cost.
🦁
Devotion of HanumanHanuman — the ultimate devotee — crosses the ocean, finds Sita, burns Lanka, and sustains the army through his selfless service. He embodies Seva (service) as the highest form of dharmic action: strength placed entirely in the service of truth and justice.
⚔️
The Lanka War & Victory of GoodThe abduction of Sita by Ravana — king of Lanka, a learned Brahmin who violated dharma — represents the corruption of knowledge divorced from virtue. Ram's war against Lanka is not merely a military campaign; it is a dharmic restoration — evil, however powerful and learned, cannot endure against truth.
👑
Ram RajyaRama's rule becomes the gold standard of the just state: no subject suffers injustice, every voice is heard, and the ruler places public welfare above personal happiness. It is cited in India's Constitution debates and in Supreme Court judgments as the ideal of governance.
Moral Takeaway for Today

The Ramayana teaches that dharma demands personal sacrifice from those in power. A ruler who privileges personal comfort over public justice has already lost the mandate of dharma. For modern jurists, the lesson is clear: the rule of law must stand above convenience, relationship, and even emotion. Ravana's fate shows that knowledge and power without ethical restraint are self-destroying. The concept of Ram Rajya directly informs India's Directive Principles of State Policy — the constitutional vision of a just, welfare state.

📜 Age Three · तृतीय युग

Dvapara Yuga

द्वापर युग · The Age of Balanced and Contested Dharma

The third age. Good and evil stand at equal strength. Dharma becomes contested — not self-evident but argued, negotiated, and won through wisdom and sacrifice. This age produces the most complex, nuanced, and morally demanding epic in all of human literature: the Mahabharata.

🦚
Life of Lord KrishnaThe eighth Avatar of Vishnu — the supreme philosopher-king-friend. Unlike Rama's perfection, Krishna operates in a world of moral complexity, political intrigue, and competing loyalties. His teaching is not "be good and all will be well" but rather: understand dharma in its full complexity and act with wisdom and without attachment.
🎲
The Dice Game & Draupadi's DishonourThe rigged game of dice in which Yudhishthira gambles away his brothers, himself, and finally his wife Draupadi — and the court's silence as Draupadi is publicly humiliated — is the Mahabharata's central act of injustice. It represents the failure of institutions and bystanders in the face of power. Every observer who stayed silent was complicit.
⚔️
The Kurukshetra WarEighteen days. Eleven akshauhinis of armies destroyed. The war tests every dharmic principle — when may one kill one's own teacher? Can a righteous end justify dishonourable means? What is the duty of a soldier who knows both sides? It is the original treatise on just war theory.
📖
The Bhagavad GitaOn the first day of battle, Arjuna refuses to fight, paralysed by grief and moral confusion. Krishna's response — 18 chapters of philosophy — is the Bhagavad Gita. It covers duty, selfless action, knowledge, devotion, and the nature of the self. It remains the most read philosophical text in the world.
🕊️
Shantiparva — Bhishma's Final CounselMortally wounded on his arrows, Bhishma waits to die, and Yudhishthira approaches to learn the art of governance. Bhishma's vast discourse on kingship, justice, economics, and ethics — the Shantiparva — is one of the world's oldest and most complete treatises on political philosophy.
Moral Takeaway for Today

The Dvapara Yuga teaches that when dharma becomes contested, institutions must be strong enough to resist the powerful. The silence of Bhishma, Drona, and Vidura during Draupadi's humiliation is the eternal warning against institutional cowardice — when those with authority choose neutrality over justice, evil wins. For modern constitutionalism, the Mahabharata's central question — what must the individual do when the state itself is unjust? — maps directly onto the jurisprudence of civil disobedience, judicial review, and the right to resist tyranny.

⏳ The Present Age · कलि युग

Kali Yuga

कलि युग · The Age of Discord & Materialism

The fourth and present age, begun after the departure of Lord Krishna (traditionally ~3102 BCE). Dharma stands on only one leg: truth, barely. The age is characterised by the ascendancy of materialism, the shortening of life, the erosion of virtue, and the corruption of institutions. And yet — it also holds a unique promise.

🚶
Departure of Krishna & Onset of KaliAs Krishna departed the world, the age of discord descended. The Pandavas renounced their kingdom and undertook the Mahaprasthana (great departure). Parikshit, the last Kuru king, encountered Kali personified trying to harm a cow — the first symbolic act of the new age: the powerful preying on the defenceless.
📉
Decline of Dharma in InstitutionsThe Puranas describe Kali Yuga as an age where rulers become thieves, priests become merchants, and merchants become robbers. Truthfulness, compassion, cleanliness, and charity — the four pillars of dharma — progressively weaken. This is not merely prophecy; it is a framework for recognising and resisting institutional decay.
⚡
The Promise of Kali YugaParadoxically, the texts also say that in Kali Yuga, liberation is more accessible than in any other age — because even a small sincere effort yields great fruit. The darkness makes even a small lamp visible from far. It is the age of maximum challenge and maximum opportunity for genuine dharmic action.
🐴
The Coming of KalkiAt the nadir of the age — when darkness is total, when kings openly rob their people, when dharma has been all but extinguished — Vishnu descends as Kalki: mounted on a white horse, sword in hand, to destroy the forces of adharma and restart the great cycle. The age ends not in despair but in renewal.
Moral Takeaway for Today

The Kali Yuga framework is the most immediately applicable of all the Yugas. We are living it. The Puranic warning — that when institutions designed to protect the weak instead serve the powerful, the foundations of civilisation are in danger — is the central challenge of modern constitutional democracy. The promise is equally clear: even in the darkest age, a single individual who upholds dharma with courage sets off a chain reaction. Every honest judge, every truthful witness, every citizen who refuses to accept injustice silently, is an act of resistance against Kali. Nyay Mitra is built in this spirit.

🌀

Timeless Wisdom · शाश्वत ज्ञान

Applying the Yuga Framework Today

The Yuga cycle is not merely a cosmological theory. It is a living map of the dynamics of justice, power, and institutional integrity across time. The four Yugas describe four stages in the decay of trust — and the necessary response to each stage.

In Satya Yuga, truth is self-evident. In Treta Yuga, it requires the courage of one ideal king. In Dvapara Yuga, it requires the sacrifice of an entire generation. In Kali Yuga — our age — it requires every individual to become a guardian of dharma in their own sphere, however small.

The most important insight the texts offer: the arc of the moral universe is long, but it bends toward truth — because the cycle always returns to Satya. Justice, ultimately, is not optional. It is cosmological.
